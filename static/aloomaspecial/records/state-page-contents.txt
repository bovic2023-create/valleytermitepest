Why Does a Home's Build Era Decide Its Termite Risk?<h2>
Walk through #State neighborhoods and you are really walking through layers of building history. Older homes were often framed with the wood resting closer to grade, settled over decades, and developed the small cracks and gaps that give subterranean termites a quiet way in. Many were built before soil pre-treatment was standard, so the only protection they ever had is whatever someone added later, if anyone did.
Newer #State homes have their own weak spot. A lot of them got a pre-construction soil treatment that was never meant to last forever. That chemical barrier ages out, and once it thins, the slab that was supposed to be protected becomes an open door at every plumbing penetration. Same street, different decade, completely different exposure. We treat the home in front of us, not the average of the block.

What a Slab Hides That a Crawlspace Reveals<h2>
Foundation type changes everything about how termites reach a #State home and how we find them. A slab home hides its weak points. Subterranean termites slip in through tiny gaps where pipes pass through the concrete, travel up inside the walls, and can feed for a long time before anything shows at eye level. There is no open space underneath to inspect, so we read the perimeter, the penetrations, and the moisture instead.
A crawlspace home shows more, but it also offers termites a sheltered highway. The piers, the sill plates, and the floor joists sit in a dim, humid space that termites love, and mud tubes climbing a pier are often the first hard proof of activity. We get under there and check it directly. Basement homes bring their own entry points where the foundation meets the framing. Knowing which of these we are dealing with is the difference between a real inspection and a hopeful one.
