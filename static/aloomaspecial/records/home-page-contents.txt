What Is a Termite Colony Doing Before You Ever See One?<h2>
A subterranean colony lives down in the soil and climbs into a home through pencil-width tunnels packed from dirt, chewed wood, and saliva. Those tunnels, called mud tubes, keep the workers from drying out as they shuttle between the ground and their food. You tend to find them running up a foundation wall, a pier, or the back of a baseboard you never think to check.
The colony itself can take three to five years to grow strong enough to send out swarmers, the winged reproductives that fly off to start new nests. So when you finally see a swarm against a window or a little drift of discarded wings on the sill, that is not the start of your trouble. It is the colony announcing that it is mature, established, and ready to spread. We treat that moment as the alarm it really is.

Identify the Species Before You Treat Anything<h2>
Different termites live in completely different ways, and that single fact changes the whole job. Subterranean termites nest in the ground and depend on soil contact, so the fix is a treated zone in the earth that the colony has to cross to reach the house. Drywood termites live their whole lives sealed inside the wood with no link to the soil at all, which calls for a different answer entirely, sometimes a localized treatment and sometimes a whole-structure one.
Guess the species wrong and you can treat a house for weeks without ever touching the colony doing the chewing. That is why our first visit is a diagnosis, not a pitch. We tell you what you have, where it sits, and what ending it will genuinely take.

The Difference Between Killing Termites and Ending a Colony<h2>
This is the center of what we do. A repellent spray might drop the termites you can see and shove the rest deeper into the walls. We reach for non-repellent products instead. The termites cannot detect them, so they stroll straight through the treated zone, carry the material home on their bodies, and pass it through the colony during the constant feeding and grooming termites do. The nest collapses from the inside. That is the whole point: not a body count at the surface, but the end of the engine that keeps sending more.
We pair that with the step most companies skip past: fixing whatever invited the termites in the first place. A leaking spigot, mulch heaped against the siding, a scrap board left lying in the dirt, water pooling by the foundation. Treat the colony and ignore those conditions, and all you have really done is schedule the next infestation.
